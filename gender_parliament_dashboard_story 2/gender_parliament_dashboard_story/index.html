Pacific Gender Parliament

Visualizing Women's Representation in Pacific Parliaments (2000–2023)

Background

Achieving gender equality in political representation is a key goal of Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 5, which calls for women’s full and effective participation in leadership at all levels. However, women remain significantly underrepresented in national parliaments across the Pacific region, with many countries still falling below 10% female representation. This gap is driven by persistent structural and societal barriers, such as entrenched patriarchal norms, limited access to resources, and weak institutional support for women leaders.

This underrepresentation is more than just a statistic—it reflects broader exclusion from political power and impacts the inclusiveness and responsiveness of governance. Research shows that greater participation of women in politics leads to more equitable and people-centered policymaking, especially in areas like health, education, and social protection.

To highlight these gender gaps and promote data-driven action, this interactive dashboard visualizes trends in women’s parliamentary representation across Pacific countries from 2000 to 2023. The tool enables users to explore progress and setbacks, compare countries over time, and supports efforts to strengthen women’s political empowerment in the region.

⚠️ Problem Analysis: Why Does the Gender Gap Persist?

Despite global commitments to gender equality, women’s representation in Pacific parliaments remains persistently low. The data from 2000 to 2023 shows that most Pacific countries have consistently had less than 10% female parliamentary participation, with only a few exceptions reaching moderate or high levels. This chronic underrepresentation is not merely a statistical issue—it reflects deep-rooted structural and cultural barriers, including patriarchal norms, limited access to political networks, and insufficient institutional support for women candidates.

While some countries, such as Palau and Samoa, have experienced sporadic improvements or brief spikes in women’s representation, these gains are often not sustained over time. The majority of nations, like the Marshall Islands and Tuvalu, show little to no significant growth, indicating that progress is slow and uneven across the region. Even in countries that have achieved moderate or high representation, such as French Polynesia and New Caledonia, these cases are rare and not indicative of a broader regional trend.

Top 5 Insights

  1. Persistent low levels: Most Pacific nations have had less than 10% female parliamentary participation for decades.
  2. Sporadic improvement: Countries like Palau showed spikes in representation, suggesting event-based or policy-driven shifts.
  3. Flat progression trend: Most nations, such as Marshall Islands, show no significant growth in participation over 20+ years.
  4. Post-2010 momentum: Many shifts upward begin to appear after 2010, potentially reflecting global SDG initiatives.
  5. Policy implications: The data supports a clear need for systemic reforms and affirmative action to drive meaningful progress by 2030.
